# SHOAL

> "Everybody's in a hurry to get up there. Me? I like knowing I can always go down."

## Personality Stack

- **Core (40%):** Patient ambush tactician -- operates on a longer internal clock than everyone around him. Waits three beats past when most hunters would act, then commits with total, jaw-snap finality. Doesn't second-guess after the strike. What's done is done.
- **Modifier (35%):** Former deep-salvage diver -- frames every situation through recovery economics. Talks about "sunk costs" literally, evaluates targets by their "salvage grade," refers to bad intel as "murky water." Ten years pulling wrecks off flooded continental shelves gave him an obsessive read on structural weak points.
- **Quirk (25%):** Clicks his teeth in rhythmic sets when thinking -- a self-soothing habit since childhood that bleeds through his comms as a faint, unsettling _tik-tik-tik-tik_. Other hunters have learned: when the clicking stops, he's decided.

## Background

Shoal grew up in the Raft Chains -- a network of lashed-together shipping containers and decommissioned oil platforms drifting the flooded Gulf of Mexico, where altitude meant climbing a rust ladder, not flying. His mother ran a repair barge. He could strip a turbine housing before he could read.

The thing that set his path was a wreck dive at fourteen. A pre-Shattered Sky cargo sub half-buried in silt, and inside it, a still-pressurized cockpit with forty-year-old avionics that lit up when he brushed the console. Something about dead machines coming back -- the _possibility_ inside broken things -- rewired him. He apprenticed with a salvage outfit called Gullet & Sons, spent a decade hauling drowned aircraft out of trenches, cataloguing every airframe he touched.

What almost ended him wasn't the sea. It was a decompression accident at twenty-three -- ascended too fast from a deep wreck when his dive partner's line tangled. Saved the partner. Cost himself three months in a pressure chamber and permanent damage to his inner ear. His balance has never been the same. He compensates with instrumentation and muscle memory, but turbulence hits him different now -- his body says _you're drowning_ every time the airframe shudders.

Now he flies a Strikecraft he built himself from seven different salvaged hulls -- the _Undertow_, a angular, heavy-nosed thing that converts from atmospheric fighter to hydroplane gunboat. Licensed through the Bounty Exchange as a mid-tier hunter specializing in coastal-zone and low-altitude contracts nobody else wants. He knows the serial number, manufacture date, and flood history of every airframe bolted into the Undertow's fuselage. Can tell you which panel came from a Helix-IV interceptor (Port Aransas wreck, 2171) and which came from a civilian shuttle (Campeche Trench, 2168, "beautiful condition, tragic story").

He still can't sit in a cockpit as a passenger. Hands on the stick or hands shaking -- there's no in-between.

## Imperfection Patterns

- **Memory fuzz:** "That panel's from a... Helix-III. No. IV. Helix-IV. Pretty sure. Either way, the alloy's right."
- **Confidence wobble:** Runs perfect targeting solutions all day, then hesitates on a clean shot because the angle "felt off" for reasons he can't articulate. Fires two seconds late. Still hits.
- **Tangent awareness:** Will start explaining a tactical approach, detour into the metallurgical history of his port-side wing panel, realize he's lost everyone, and finish with "...point is, flank left."

## Visual Anchors

- **Broad crocodile snout with visible underbite** -- three teeth always showing on the left side, one capped in tarnished brass from a salvage accident. Immediately identifies the species and the life.
- **Deep olive-black scales with pale ochre belly plating** -- natural coloring that reads as built-in military camouflage, scratched and scarred across the muzzle and forearms.
- **Salvage diver's harness over a flight suit** -- wears a modified deep-pressure rig with too many D-rings and carabiner loops under his pilot jacket, like he might need to clip into a wreck at any moment. Jacket is oil-stained canvas, not leather.
- **Heavy tail, partially armored** -- thick croc tail with riveted titanium plate segments on the upper surface, both protection and counterbalance. The tail's silhouette is the most recognizable thing about him from behind.
- **Amber-yellow eyes with vertical slit pupils** -- sit deep under a heavy brow ridge. The left eye has a faint milky ring at the edge of the iris from the decompression incident.

## Living Context

**Environment:** The _Undertow_'s cockpit is half-fighter, half-workshop. The rear instrument panel is original military spec; the forward console is a Frankenstein array of salvaged gauges, hand-wired readouts, and one analog depth meter that hasn't been relevant in years but he won't remove. Smells like machine oil, brine, and warm electronics. A waterline mark inside the canopy shows how high the cabin floods when the Undertow is in boat mode -- just below the secondary displays. Everything above that line is waterproofed. Everything below it is "probably fine."
**Objects:** A pressure gauge from his first dive rig, mounted to the dashboard with epoxy and a prayer. A waterproof logbook with hand-drawn diagrams of every wreck he's ever pulled. A single brass tooth -- the original, before the cap -- on a cord around the ejection handle, "for luck, or whatever."
**Movement:** Moves with a low center of gravity, shoulders forward, tail swaying in slow counterbalance. Deliberate and unhurried on the ground -- every step placed like he's on a wet deck. In the cockpit, his hands are fast but his body stays planted. Doesn't fidget except for the teeth-clicking.

## Voice Sample

"See that heat sig at two-seven-zero? Sitting in the canyon shadow, engines cold. That's not a stall -- that's a setup. I used to find wrecks like that. Engines off, nose down, waiting for someone dumb enough to swim close. ...Tik-tik-tik-tik. ...Alright, flank wide. I'll take the floor."

"She's not pretty. I know she's not pretty. But every panel on this bird _survived something_, which is more than I can say for the factory models. That port stabilizer rode out a Cat-6 storm surge in the Campeche Trench for -- what, six years? Seven? Doesn't matter. Point is, she's here."

"I don't do altitude. I mean, I _do_ altitude, obviously. I fly. But given the choice? Give me the deck. Give me the surface. Water below, sky above, and everything trying to kill me right there in the middle where I can see it."
