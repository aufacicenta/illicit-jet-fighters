# Peleanu Crater Complex
> "The Dish" — a perfectly round kill-bowl where everyone can see you and nobody can hide

## Environment Stack
- **Terrain (40%):** Glassed impact crater — a 12km-diameter perfect circle punched into the Pacific shelf, shallow enough that the ocean floor is visible through turquoise water at low tide, deep enough to swallow a Strikecraft at high
- **Modifier (35%):** Rotating ion shear — a permanent cyclonic charge differential spirals around the crater rim like a slow clock hand, frying unshielded avionics and bending guided ordnance into corkscrews
- **Quirk (25%):** The water reflects the sky so perfectly at dawn that pilots lose horizon reference — instruments say level, eyes say inverted, and the crater becomes a sphere of blue with no up

## History

Peleanu Atoll was a mid-Pacific oceanographic research station built on a coral ring, home to forty marine biologists and a deepwater sonar array the military pretended wasn't theirs. Unremarkable. Funded quietly. The kind of place that showed up on charts as a dot and in budgets as a rounding error.

During the Shattered Sky, a kinetic rod — one of the unattributed ones nobody claims credit for — punched through the fractured atmosphere and struck the atoll dead center. The coral ring vaporized. The ocean floor fused into a glass bowl. The sonar array's power core cracked and has been leaking ionized particulate into the water table ever since, feeding a permanent electromagnetic cyclone that orbits the crater rim like a searchlight made of static.

What moved in was inevitable: the crater sits on the seam between three contested air corridors, and its perfectly circular geometry makes it a natural dueling ground. The Bounty Exchange designated it a sanctioned engagement zone within two years. No faction controls it. No faction wants to — holding the Dish means sitting in a fishbowl while every hunter in the Pacific watches your radar signature bounce off glass water. It's a place people go to settle things fast, in the open, where the only advantage is being better than the other pilot.

Hunters call it "The Dish" because that's what it is — a satellite dish pointed at God, and you're the signal.

## Terrain Features

### The Glass Floor ("The Mirror")
- **What it is:** The fused crater bed sits 2–8 meters below sea level depending on tide. At low tide, it's a shimmering turquoise lens. At high tide, it's a flat ocean surface with no reference points. The glass is slightly concave — everything rolls toward center.
- **Tactical role:** No cover. Total exposure. But the reflective surface doubles every visual contact — pilots see ghost images below them, making target acquisition a puzzle of real-versus-reflection at low altitude.
- **Risk/reward:** Flying low over the Mirror gains you a visual duplicate that confuses pursuing missile locks. But misjudge altitude by three meters and you eat glass-salt water at 600 knots.

### The Rim ("The Ring")
- **What it is:** The crater edge forms a perfect circle of shattered coral and fused rock, rising 40–60 meters above sea level. Smooth on the inner face, jagged on the outer. No gaps, no passes — a continuous wall-less lip you fly over or under, never through.
- **Tactical role:** The only altitude reference in the arena. Crossing the Rim means entering or leaving the Dish, and the ion shear hits hardest directly above it. It's a commitment line — once you drop inside, climbing back out costs you seconds in the shear.
- **Risk/reward:** Hugging the Rim's inner curve lets you orbit the arena edge with a constant escape vector. But the shear is strongest here, and your instruments will lie to you for 3–5 seconds on every pass.

### The Navel ("Dead Drop")
- **What it is:** The exact center of the crater, where the old sonar array's power core sits cracked open on the glass floor. A 200-meter radius of water that glows faint green from ionized particulate leakage. Visible from every angle.
- **Tactical role:** The electromagnetic bleed creates a sensor dead zone — radar returns nothing inside the Navel. You vanish from instruments. So does everyone else.
- **Risk/reward:** Entering the Navel makes you invisible to guided weapons and radar locks. But you're also blind — no instruments, no lock capability, pure visual flight in a green haze. And every pilot in the Dish knows exactly where you went.

### Tide Shelves ("The Steps")
- **What it is:** Three concentric rings where the glass floor steps up in shallow terraces, each about 1.5 meters higher than the last, visible as color-bands in the water — dark blue, teal, turquoise. They circle the crater at roughly 2km, 4km, and 5.5km from center.
- **Tactical role:** The only terrain variation in the bowl. At low tide, the outer shelf breaks the surface and spray kicks up behind low-flying craft — a visual trail that marks your position but also creates a momentary IR-confusing mist cloud behind you.
- **Risk/reward:** Using the spray as improvised chaff works exactly once per pass. After that, the pursuing pilot expects it. But the shelf edges create micro-turbulence that can shake a lock if you time your jink to the step.

### Thermocline Columns ("Steam Stacks")
- **What it is:** Four points where subsurface thermal vents hit the shallow water and send columns of steam 80–120 meters into the air. They shift position slightly with current but stay roughly equidistant around the crater, about 3km from center. Each column is 30–40 meters wide.
- **Tactical role:** The only vertical visual obstruction in the arena. Flying through a Steam Stack breaks line of sight for 0.5–1 second and dumps IR-hot moisture across your airframe, temporarily masking your thermal signature.
- **Risk/reward:** Threading a Stack at combat speed is a skill check — the steam is turbulent, visibility drops to zero inside, and you exit wet and disoriented. But a well-timed Stack transit can break a missile lock or set up a reversal. The columns are destructible in a sense — a sustained weapons pass can temporarily disperse one for 20–30 seconds before it rebuilds.

## Sensory Profile

| Sense | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Sight** | Overwhelming blue. The crater is a circle of water inside a circle of white coral inside a circle of deep ocean. At dawn, the reflection kills the horizon and the arena becomes a sphere. At noon, the Glass Floor turns the whole bowl into a lens — light comes from below as much as above. The Steam Stacks are the only vertical interruption, white columns against blue everything. |
| **Sound** | The tide hums. A low, resonant throb every 90 seconds that vibrates through the canopy frame. Weapons fire echoes off the glass floor and comes back a half-second later — every shot sounds doubled. Comms carry a constant static undertone from the ion bleed, worse near the Rim. |
| **Feel** | The air is wet and warm. Turbulence is minimal except near the Stacks and the Rim — the bowl shape creates laminar flow across most of the arena, making it deceptively smooth. | **Smell** | Ozone and brine. The ion shear cooks the salt air and it leaks through every seal. After ten minutes in the Dish, everything tastes like copper and seawater. Near the Navel, the ionized particulate adds a chemical sweetness that veteran pilots associate with danger. |
| **Radar** | Clean as a lab bench outside the shear arc — the glass bowl and flat water create perfect returns with zero ground clutter. Too clean. Every craft in the arena is a bright, unambiguous contact. The Navel is a perfect black circle on scope. The shear arc reads as a slow-moving wall of noise. Pilots used to cluttered urban environments find the Dish's radar clarity disorienting — there's nowhere for a signal to hide. |

## Engagement Zones

### The Bowl (0–50m altitude, inside the Rim)
- **Altitude band / area:** Surface level to fifty meters above the water, spanning the full crater interior
- **Character:** Knife-fight territory. The Mirror doubles every visual contact, the tidal surge reshapes the floor beneath you, and there's zero cover. Engagements here are fast, visual, and often mutual — both pilots firing at the same time because neither can break contact.
- **Who thrives here:** Speed-and-guns builds. Pilots with strong visual acquisition and dumb-fire weapons who don't need radar locks. Aircraft with low IR signatures that blend into the water reflection.
- **Transition threat:** Climbing out of the Bowl means crossing through the mid-altitude band where you're silhouetted against the water from above and the sky from below — a perfect target sandwich.

### The Gallery (50–200m altitude, inside the Rim)
- **Altitude band / area:** Mid-altitude band above the water surface, where most of the Steam Stacks live
- **Character:** The maneuvering layer. Enough altitude to use the Stacks for cover transitions, enough room to set up attack runs into the Bowl. Most sustained dogfights happen here — it's where positioning matters more than pure speed.
- **Who thrives here:** All-rounders and ambush pilots. Craft with good turn rates and guided weapons who can use the Stacks to break and re-engage. Pilots who've memorized the shear timing and use it as a shield.
- **Transition threat:** Diving into the Bowl commits you to a close-range fight. Climbing above the Rim exposes you to the full force of the ion shear and makes you visible to anyone within 50 kilometers of the crater.

### The Crown (Rim altitude, ~40–80m above sea level)
- **Altitude band / area:** The narrow band directly above and around the crater Rim itself
- **Character:** The commitment zone. Flying the Crown means orbiting the arena's edge with one wing over the bowl and one over open ocean. The shear is strongest here. Every four minutes, the Hand passes through and you either dodge it or eat static for eight seconds.
- **Who thrives here:** Patient hunters and long-range specialists who want to pick their moment. From the Crown, you can see the entire arena below — every contact, every Stack, every tide phase. But you're also the most visible target in the sky.
- **Transition threat:** Dropping from Crown to Gallery is fast but predictable — defenders in the Gallery see you coming. And if the Hand catches you mid-dive, you enter the Gallery blind and deaf.

### Open Ocean (outside the Rim, any altitude)
- **Altitude band / area:** Beyond the crater edge in every direction — flat, featureless Pacific
- **Character:** Reset space. Pilots who need to break contact, repair systems, or re-plan exit the Dish into open ocean. There's nothing here — no terrain, no cover, no features. Just distance.
- **Who thrives here:** Nobody fights here by choice. But damaged craft or pilots waiting for the Hand to pass use it as a staging area. Some hunters orbit outside, waiting for a wounded pilot to flee the Rim.
- **Transition threat:** Re-entering the Dish means crossing the Rim and the shear. Every re-entry is announced — your radar return lights up the bowl the moment you crest the lip.
